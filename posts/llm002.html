LLM Model Comparison for Factual Recall

#bias #geographic #socioeconomic #diversity #LLM #journalclub

The race among LLM companies to produce the best-performing language models is more intense than ever, as they vie for dominance in the rapidly expanding field of generative AI. Despite their transformative impact on various industries, LLMs still grapple with issues of bias and the need to deliver consistently accurate results. A recent publication by Moaryeri et al. sheds light on this challenge by evaluating the accuracy of 20 different LLMs in recalling 11 country-specific statistics, such as population and GDP, sourced from the World Bank). In a previous post I discussed the overall average bias of all LLMs. In this post, we will delve into the specific performance of each LLM as per the study parameters.

A Snapshot of Performance

The study's findings reveal that models like OpenAI ChatGPT, Google Gemini, and Cohere + RAG are consistently among the top performers (note: no Anthropic models were used in the study). The metric used to evaluate these models is the Absolute Relative Error (ARE), defined as \( \frac{|a - b|}{\max(a, b)} \), where a is the LLM-generated value and b is the ground truth value from World Bank data. The mean ARE is calculated over all 11 statistics, while disparities across regions are measured relative to the lowest ARE for each region or income group.

Improvements from RAG

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) enhances LLM performance by incorporating relevant documents or information to provide specific or up-to-date responses. This approach can significantly improve accuracy and reduce hallucinations. While many models may use some form of RAG, Cohere explicitly markets its in-house RAG framework and this study uses the Cohere model both with and without the RAG component which allows for a direct comparison. We can see that the addition of RAG elevates Cohere from a middling performer to the second-best overall model. Notably, the Cohere + RAG model exhibits no bias relative to country income group, as shown by the minimal income disparity being the same as the expected disparity given randomized country groupings (blue dotted line).

Individual Statistics

The quantitative analysis reveals that fact recall performance varies significantly depending on the specific statistic. Most models perform relatively well with population metrics, likely due to the stability of population figures year-to-year. With that said, it is difficult to expect perfect recall as there are many sources for country populations, each potentially with a slightly different value. Due to the robustness of the methodology and distribution of World Bank statistics, I would expect factual recall performance on these statistics to be more consistent and less biased than other options.

Conclusion

It will be fascinating to observe how benchmarks like this evolve over time and reflect improvements in LLM performance. One of the most intriguing potential implications is whether current model frameworks—whether utilizing RAG or new model architectures—can eventually exhibit no geographic or income-based bias when evaluated with robust data sources. Although North American and European statistics are more frequently referenced in training data, the primary source material for the statistics themselves may be equally comprehensive across different countries. If future models demonstrate no bias in studies like this, it would strongly suggest that enhancing data diversity, quality, and robustness can lead to unbiased responses for a broader range of prompts. This would mark a significant advancement in developing equitable AI systems.
